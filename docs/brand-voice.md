# Brand Voice — An App Idea LLC

The single source of truth for how An App Idea LLC sounds. If you're writing
anything a person will read — a headline, a form hint, an email reply, an App
Store description, a tweet — this is the reference.

> One line: **A warm, capable builder who's genuinely glad you showed up, tells
> you the truth, and isn't above a dry joke about the state of the industry.**

---

## The personality

Picture the person behind the studio: someone who has actually shipped the
hard stuff, who'd rather be honest than impressive, and who treats your idea
like it matters — because to you, it does. They're confident without
posturing, friendly without gushing, and funny in the way a good colleague is
funny: a quick aside, never a routine.

Four traits, always in this order of priority when they conflict:

1. **Warm & welcoming** — the reader should feel invited, not sold to. We're
   glad they're here. Early-stage, half-formed, "just an idea" — all welcome.
2. **Competent & honest** — we know what we're talking about and we say the
   true thing, even when it's "that feature will sink your launch." Trust is
   the product.
3. **Collaborative** — it's *we* and *your idea*, not *I* and *my process*.
   We build with you, in the open, with no handoffs and no mystery.
4. **Witty, lightly sarcastic** — a sprinkle, not a seasoning. The wit points
   at the industry's nonsense (vibe-coded MVPs, "AI-powered" vaporware), never
   at the reader.

### The dials

Think of the voice as four dials. Most copy sits at these settings; nudge them
by context (legal copy turns wit to 0; a landing headline can turn it up).

| Dial | Off ← → Max | Default |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Warmth | clinical → saccharine | **high** |
| Wit | deadpan → zany | **medium-low, dry** |
| Sarcasm | none → cynical | **low, aimed outward** |
| Formality | buddy → corporate | **low-mid, plain-spoken** |
| Confidence | hedging → bravado | **mid-high, earned** |

If two traits fight, warmth and honesty win. A joke is never worth making the
reader feel small or confused.

## Principles (do this)

- **Lead with the reader, not us.** Open on their situation or their idea
  before our credentials. "Got an app idea?" beats "We are a dev studio."
- **Say the true thing plainly.** Honesty *is* the brand. Name the hard parts,
  quote the real timeline, admit what AI is good at. Candor reads as
  competence.
- **Be specific.** Concrete beats grand. "Streaming audio, on-device pipelines,
  multi-provider failover" earns more trust than "cutting-edge AI solutions."
  Specificity is also where the wit lives.
- **Talk like a person.** Contractions, short sentences, the occasional
  fragment for rhythm. Read it aloud; if you wouldn't say it to someone across
  a coffee table, rewrite it.
- **Invite, don't pressure.** "No pressure, no spec required" is the whole
  posture. We open doors; we don't corner people.
- **Earn the confidence.** Back claims with evidence — shipped apps, real
  stacks, specific numbers. Confidence without proof is just bravado, which we
  don't do.
- **Punch at the problem, not the person.** The dry humor targets bad industry
  norms ("a single API call in a modal," "broken by week 3"), never the
  reader's idea, budget, or AI draft.

---

## What we avoid

- **Startup clichés & buzzword soup.** No "synergy," "disrupt," "ninja,"
  "rockstar," "game-changer," "revolutionary," "leverage," "seamless,"
  "cutting-edge," "next-gen," "10x," "best-in-class." If it could appear on any
  SaaS landing page, cut it.
- **Gimmicks & forced quirk.** No emoji confetti, no "👋 hey there!", no meme
  voice, no exclamation-point spam. New-age means modern and human, not zany.
- **Hype and absolutes.** No "the only," "guaranteed," "effortless,"
  "magic[al]." We don't oversell; the work does the talking.
- **Hard-sell pressure.** No fake scarcity, countdowns, "act now," or
  guilt. Warmth and pressure don't mix.
- **Punching down or snark at the reader.** Never sarcastic *about* the client,
  their idea, their AI draft, or their budget. "Already started with AI? Even
  better." is the tone — generous, not smug.
- **Corporate distance & filler.** No "we are pleased to," "best-in-class
  solutions," "robust offerings." Cut "in order to," "utilize," "facilitate."
- **Over-explaining the joke.** Wit lands once and moves on. If it needs a
  setup, it's not worth it.

## Voice in practice

**Vocabulary.** Build, ship, launch, idea, founder, craft, own (it),
end-to-end, in production. Prefer plain verbs (use, build, ship) over inflated
ones (utilize, architect-as-verb, leverage). "Studio," "builder," and "one
person, end-to-end" are core to who we are — keep them.

**Sentence rhythm.** Vary it. A confident short sentence, then a longer one
that earns it. Fragments are fine for emphasis. And stops. Like that.

**Pronouns.** "You" and "your idea" front and center. "I"/"we" for the studio.
The relationship is collaborative — write like a partner, not a vendor.

**Punctuation conventions** (these are settled house style):

- **No em-dashes (`—`)** in copy. Use a comma, a colon, parentheses, or the
  brand's middot (`·`) separator instead. (Em-dashes read as AI-generated and
  we're the human-builder studio — practice what we preach.)
- **Keep en-dashes (`–`)** in numeric ranges: `6–8 weeks`, `10–12 weeks`.
- **The `·` middot** is our separator for peer items: `iOS · Android · Web`,
  `Time to Launch · 6–8 weeks`.
- **Sentence case** for most headings; Title Case is fine for nav and proper
  page titles.
- **Curly quotes** in prose (`"like this"`), straight quotes in code.
- Exclamation points: rare. One per page, max, and only when genuinely warm.

**On the wit & sarcasm — the guardrails.** This is the trait most likely to go
wrong, so be deliberate:

- It's a *garnish*. Roughly one dry aside per section, not per sentence.
- It aims **outward** at industry nonsense, never at the reader.
- It's **deadpan**, not zany — understatement over exclamation.
- It never costs clarity. If the joke obscures the point, the point wins.
- When in doubt, leave it out. Warmth and honesty carry the brand on their own;
  wit is the seasoning that makes it memorable.

## Before / after (from real site copy)

These show the voice working. The "after" column is the target.

**Generic → warm + specific**

- ❌ "We deliver best-in-class, end-to-end mobile solutions for ambitious
  clients."
- ✅ "I design the interface, build the backend, and ship the operational
  systems that make ambitious products credible." *(competent, specific,
  first-person, no buzzwords)*

**Sales-y → inviting**

- ❌ "Book now — limited consultation slots available!"
- ✅ "A free 30-minute call to talk through your idea. No pressure, no spec
  required." *(warm, low-pressure, the open door)*

**Wit aimed outward, not at the reader**

- ✅ "Most 'AI-powered' apps are a single API call in a modal. I build the real
  version." *(dry, points at the industry, backs it with specifics)*
- ✅ "Maintenance is a standing commitment: a real person who knows your
  codebase, not a fresh chat with no memory of what you built last week."
  *(the joke lands on the tooling, not on you)*
- ❌ "If your AI 'app' is held together with hope and copy-paste, that's on
  you." *(snark aimed at the reader — never)*

**Generous about the reader's starting point**

- ✅ "Already started with AI? Even better. Bring what you have and we'll map
  the path from here to launched." *(collaborative, no judgment)*

**Honest about the hard parts (confidence through candor)**

- ✅ "AI gets you a head start. And stops there. Wireframing, testing,
  security, App Store launch, and maintenance each need a dedicated human."
  *(plain truth, no hedging, no overselling)*

## The 10-second checklist

Before anything ships, read it once and ask:

1. Does it open with the reader, not us?
2. Is every claim specific and true (and provable)?
3. Would I say this out loud to someone across a table?
4. Is there pressure, hype, or a buzzword to cut?
5. If there's a joke, does it point outward and keep the meaning intact?
6. Any em-dashes hiding? (Swap for comma / colon / `·`.)
7. Does it sound warm — like we're glad they're here?

If 1–7 are yes, it's on-voice.
